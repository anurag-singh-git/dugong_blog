## New post

**Dispatch Nº 59 — "The reorder you always make too late"**
`the-reorder-you-always-make-too-late.html` · by Priya Singh · 2026-06-15 · ~1,860 words declared

Chosen because supplier replenishment (reorder points + purchase orders) is a heavily
complained-about, well-upvoted Shopify pain that no native feature closes, and it was not yet
covered by the existing 12 dispatches. It is distinct from the three adjacent inventory posts:
the restock post (Nº 56) is the customer-facing back-in-stock waitlist, the overselling post is
real-time count accuracy across channels, and the sold-out post is on-site merchandising of
items already out of stock. This post is the upstream, supplier-side decision: when and how much
to buy back, before anything runs out.

The Shopify-specific limitations are confirmed from current research and form the crux:
(1) Shopify tracks inventory and offers a purchase order screen but has no engine that reads
sales and stock, decides what to reorder and how much, and drafts the PO; merchants on the
community forums repeatedly call the native PO "too basic." (2) There is no per-SKU reorder
point, let alone one derived from sell-through and supplier lead time. (3) The PO does not
auto-populate the product cost already on file, there is no native field for the supplier's own
SKU, and there is no real reporting on what is on the way / partially received / pending, so
nothing connects the on-hand count to the lead-time calendar. These are all current,
repeatedly-raised merchant complaints.

Statistics are sourced from current web research, not invented, and presented as
approximations/ranges in the body: stockouts cost retailers ~$1.2 trillion a year in lost sales
worldwide; ~8% of SKUs are out of stock at any moment; ~half of products hit at least one
stockout a year; the average stockout lasts ~35 days; ~43% of shoppers go to a competitor when
an item is unavailable.

The angle beyond "install an app": replenishment is a forecasting-and-timing problem, not a
data-entry problem. The real job is to project days of cover per SKU from recent pace, trend,
and seasonality, subtract each supplier's real lead time to find the must-reorder-by date,
right-size the buy against both stockouts and idle cash, group items by supplier to clear order
minimums, draft the PO with the right cost and supplier SKU pre-filled, flag spikes and imminent
stockouts, and hand the drafted orders to the merchant to approve before anything sends. A
callout warns that overordering is the same planning failure pointed the other way (frozen cash,
storage, markdown risk), so the goal is the right quantity at the right time, not "never run out."
