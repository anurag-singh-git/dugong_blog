## New post

**Dispatch Nº 57 — "The sold-out products that sink your collection"**
`the-sold-out-products-that-sink-your-collection.html` · by Priya Singh · 2026-06-13 · ~1,820 words declared

Chosen because out-of-stock merchandising (sold-out products clogging the top of collection and
search pages) is one of the longest-running and most-upvoted Shopify community feature requests,
and it was not yet covered by the existing nine dispatches. It is deliberately distinct from the
two adjacent inventory posts: the restock post (Nº 56) is about notifying the waitlist customer
after a stockout, and the overselling post is about inventory accuracy at checkout. This post is
about the storefront itself: the dead tile that greets browsing traffic and drags down the
conversion of every in-stock product behind it.

The Shopify-specific limitation is confirmed from current research and is the crux of the piece:
Shopify has no reliable native sort that pushes out-of-stock products to the bottom of a whole
collection. Some sort options and theme code push sold-out items down within a single page, but
once pagination kicks in each page sorts itself, so a later page can be all sold-out while the
first is mixed; Shopify's Liquid sort filter has no `inventory_quantity` parameter, so theme code
cannot sort cleanly across the collection either. The only native reliable option is manual
drag-to-reorder, which does not scale. Hiding via a smart-collection "inventory greater than 0"
condition is all-or-nothing: it works only on automated collections (not manual ones), does not
apply to site search, and removes the product entirely, throwing away its SEO and the back-in-stock
signup.

Statistics are sourced from current web research, not invented: stockouts account for ~40% of lost
sales; nearly 7 in 10 (~69%) shoppers leave without buying when they cannot find what they came
for; ~51% of products hit at least one stockout a year; a stocked-out product stays unavailable for
an average of ~35 days; the typical out-of-stock rate is ~8%, rising to ~10% on promotional items.
These are presented as ranges/approximations in the body.

The angle the piece adds beyond "install an app": the real job is merchandising judgement and
freshness, not a one-time sort. Demote freshly sold-out items below the in-stock ones rather than
deleting the page (keep the URL and the notify-me form alive), keep items restocking this week
visible with a back-soon badge, protect hero and high-ranking products from being buried, treat
search differently from a hand-curated edit, and restore original positions automatically on
restock. Two failure modes are called out that the naive fixes create: hand-sorting goes stale by
lunch and never finishes, and hiding outright forfeits SEO and the waitlist.
