## New post

**Dispatch Nº 66 — "The review request you never send"**
`the-review-request-you-never-send.html` · by Priya Singh · 2026-06-22 · ~2,010 words

Chosen because post-purchase review collection is a high-volume, frequently-complained Shopify pain
that the existing 19 dispatches had not covered. It validates strongly on public forums: the recurring
community question is, almost verbatim, "how do I automate a review request email after delivery?",
and the early answers (including from Shopify support in those threads) are a version of "you cannot do
that natively, you will need an app." The confirmed native gap is the crux: Shopify retired its own
product reviews app and never replaced it, so an out-of-the-box store has no native review collection at
all. The Shop channel gathers a store review (not a review of the item bought), and Shopify Flow can
react to an order being marked delivered but cannot write the message, time it by product, skip a
customer mid-return, follow up, or store the rating, so the real work lives in a third-party app the
merchant still has to configure.

The heading screams the use case for anyone living the pain ("the review request you never send"),
pulling merchants who know they should be collecting reviews but never get the ask out, and it carries
the high-intent searched phrase "review request."

It is deliberately adjacent to but distinct from the existing posts. It sits next to the **WISMO** post
(Nº, the other post-delivery message that reads the order and replies at the right moment) and the
**repeat-customer segmentation** post (who the customer is should change the ask), and feeds the same
flywheel as the **abandoned-cart** recovery post (a well-timed, well-judged message beats a generic
blast). The differentiator beyond "install a review app": the request that lands is not a slot to fill,
it is a read of the specific order, tying to delivery (not fulfilment), tuning the delay to the product
(consumables sooner, durables later), skipping the customer who is mid-complaint, naming the actual
product, asking once with at most one follow-up, and routing a low rating privately to support before it
lands in public.
